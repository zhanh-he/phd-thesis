\section{Chapter Overview and Position in the Thesis}
\label{sec:audiodyn:overview}

This chapter addresses the audio-only scenario introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}. Unlike the velocity-focused chapters, the target here is not note-level MIDI velocity but beat-synchronous score dynamics. As discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:background}, score dynamics is human-readable but inherently relative, and its natural coordinate is the beat grid rather than isolated note events. The goal of this chapter is therefore to recover score-level dynamic labels directly from performance audio, while jointly estimating the metrical structure needed to place those labels on a symbolic score.

This chapter occupies a distinct position in the thesis. Chapter~\ref{ch:velcomp} and Chapter~\ref{ch:scoreinf} recover note-level velocity, which is precise and directly editable. The present chapter instead targets a more interpretable score-facing representation. It therefore completes the thesis map by addressing the setting in which audio is the only available input and the desired output is a beat-synchronous symbolic description rather than raw note-level control.

\section{Task-Specific Related Work}
\label{sec:audiodyn:related}

Audio-to-score dynamics recovery is related to, but distinct from, note-level velocity estimation. A large part of the expressive-performance literature studies how performed loudness relates to symbolic dynamic markings and how those markings vary with musical context \cite{kosta2014dynpiano,kosta2016mapping,jones2023probingdyn}. This literature makes two points that matter here. First, score dynamics is musically meaningful and worth recovering. Second, it is inherently ambiguous and cannot be reduced to a fixed physical threshold.

A second relevant line of work concerns metrical structure estimation. Beat and downbeat tracking has a strong literature in MIR, with modern deep models achieving increasingly robust results \cite{bock2020deconstruct,zhao2022beat,foscarin2024beat}. For the present chapter, beat and downbeat estimation is not only an auxiliary task. It is part of the output interface because score dynamics is easiest to interpret and evaluate on a beat grid.

These observations motivate a joint rather than isolated formulation. Predicting score dynamics without metre produces labels that are hard to place. Predicting metre without dynamics misses the expressive target. The present chapter therefore studies score-facing dynamics recovery through joint estimation of dynamic levels, change points, beats, and downbeats.

\section{Problem Setting and Targets}
\label{sec:audiodyn_targets}

Chapter~\ref{ch:background} already introduced the ambiguity of score dynamics, the distinction between score dynamics and MIDI velocity, and the role of beats and downbeats as a musically meaningful coordinate. Rather than repeat that background, this chapter focuses on the concrete transcription problem studied in this work.

Given a piano performance audio signal, the system predicts four synchronized output sequences on a frame grid: dynamic levels, dynamic change points, beats, and downbeats. The dynamic targets are the score-facing outputs, while beats and downbeats provide the metrical structure required to align these labels to a symbolic score. This joint design serves two practical modes of use. In the first mode, a reliable beat grid is already available from an aligned score or a score-level AMT system, and the predicted dynamic labels are attached to that grid. In the second mode, no score is available, and the model first predicts beats and downbeats by itself, then uses the estimated grid to place the dynamic labels.

More specifically, the dynamic-level target is formulated as a six-class frame-wise prediction problem, including one \textit{blank} class before the first annotated marking and the five score-dynamics classes used in MazurkaBL: \textit{pp}, \textit{p}, \textit{mf}, \textit{f}, and \textit{ff}. The change-point target is binary and indicates whether a contextual transition between adjacent dynamic regimes occurs at a given time. Beats and downbeats are also binary frame-wise targets. At inference time, the frame-wise outputs are converted into beat-synchronous score annotations by sampling the dynamic class at beat locations and snapping detected change points to the nearest beat.

\section{Perceptually Grounded Features and Multi-Scale Backbone}
\label{sec:audiodyn_features}

\subsection{Audio feature representations}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_features}

Log-Mel spectrograms serve as the standard input representation in many modern MIR systems, providing a natural baseline for both dynamics estimation and beat/downbeat tracking tasks. To ensure a fair comparison with advanced beat tracking models \cite{zhao2022beat, foscarin2024beat}, their widely standardised audio preprocessing configuration is adopted throughout this chapter. Specifically, the audio is downmixed to mono and resampled to 22.05~kHz. A Hann-windowed STFT is then applied using a window length of 1024 and a hop size of 441 samples, followed by a projection onto 128 Mel bands. This yields a precise frame rate of 50~fps. Under this setting, the log-Mel input is represented as $\mathbf{X}_{\mathrm{Mel}} \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times T}$, where $T$ denotes the number of temporal frames after feature extraction.

Recent studies have started to question whether log-Mel features are the most suitable representation for musical dynamics. Narang et al. \cite{narang2024automatic} compared log-Mel and Bark-scale loudness-based features for singing voice dynamics estimation and reported that Bark-scale features consistently outperformed log-Mel inputs. This observation is particularly relevant to the present study because score dynamics are closer to perceived intensity than to raw spectral energy. For piano dynamics, this motivation is also consistent with the use of specific loudness in earlier studies on performed dynamics \cite{kosta2016mapping}. BSSL represents loudness over auditory critical bands, and therefore keeps the perceptual distribution of intensity across frequency regions. This makes it more informative than a single integrated loudness curve, while being more compact and perceptually aligned than a high-dimensional spectral magnitude representation.

A core challenge was the implementation of the BSSL extractor. MOSQITO \cite{san2021mosqito} provides an ISO~532-1:2017-compliant pipeline \cite{iso532_1_2017} appropriate for vocal settings \cite{narang2024automatic}, but its 48~kHz-oriented workflow is overly constrained. This is unsuitable here because the MazurkaBL dataset is natively 44.1~kHz, while the baselines for comparison are all processed under a 22.05~kHz, 50~fps feature configuration. Using MOSQITO would require an additional resampling (44.1 kHz to 48 kHz) and different FFT parameters, making the feature comparison unbalanced and less efficient. We instead implemented a custom BSSL extractor in PyTorch by reproducing the \texttt{ma\_sone} function from Pampalk's Music Analysis MATLAB toolbox \cite{pampalk2004toolbox}, following the algorithmic chain in \cite{pampalk2002content}. The mathematical formulation of this extractor is given in Chapter~\ref{ch:background}. Using the same STFT parameters as the log-Mel input, the BSSL extractor produces $\mathbf{X}_{\mathrm{BSSL}} \in \mathbb{R}^{22 \times T}$. We use the full BSSL matrix rather than compressing it into the BSTL, because the latter would discard the frequency-band structure that may carry useful timbral and register-dependent cues for piano dynamics.

\subsection{Shared multi-scale encoder}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_encoder}
A central modeling challenge is the conflict between the temporal context and frame-level precision required by the four tasks. While metrical tasks (beats and downbeats) rely on high temporal resolution to localize sharp transients \cite{bock2020deconstruct}, dynamics-related tasks (dynamics and change points) depend on the surrounding phrase, local contrast, register, and relative loudness context \cite{narang2024automatic}, thereby requiring extended context lengths and stable spectral features. We therefore adapt the multi-scale network of \cite{li2023frame} as the shared encoder. Since this architecture was originally designed for playing techniques spanning different temporal scales, it is exceptionally well-equipped to resolve the competing receptive-field requirements of our four tasks.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:audiodyn_model}, given an $F \times T$ time--frequency feature, where $F$ is the number of frequency bands and $T$ is the number of time frames, the encoder first applies 2D batch normalization and then forms three temporal branches at resolutions $T$, $T/s$, and $T/s^{2}$, where $s$ denotes the temporal scaling factor. The full-resolution branch preserves frame-level timing evidence, the middle branch provides an intermediate representation, and the coarsest branch gives the largest effective receptive field. Following \cite{li2023frame}, residual blocks are used for local feature extraction, while self-attention blocks are applied to strengthen long-range temporal modeling. After multi-scale fusion, the encoder outputs a shared latent sequence $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times 8}$, which is then passed to the MMoE decoder and task-specific heads.

Unlike the reference architecture which fixes the scaling factor to 3 \cite{li2023frame}, we treat $s$ as a tunable hyperparameter to explicitly control the trade-off between temporal precision and contextual abstraction. If $s$ is too small, the three branches become insufficiently differentiated and the encoder behaves close to a single-resolution model with redundant side branches. If $s$ is too large, the coarsest branch provides stronger context but may over-compress information that is still useful for event localization. Under our 50~fps feature rate, the empirically best value $s=5$ means that the coarsest branch operates at approximately 2~fps, giving the attention blocks a substantially longer temporal view while the full-rate branch still preserves 20~ms frame-level timing. The ablation study in Section~\ref{ssec:audiodyn_ablation} discusses this effect in more detail.

The feature choice also interacts with the model design. Using 22-band BSSL instead of 128-bin log-Mel reduces the frequency dimension entering the convolutional encoder, lowering memory usage and making longer segments feasible. This allows the model to capture phrase-level dynamics while remaining compact, as further confirmed in Section~\ref{ssec:audiodyn_ablation} Ablation Study.

\section{Joint Estimation with Task-Aware Decoding}
\label{sec:audiodyn_mtl}

\subsection{MMoE decoder}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_mmoe}

The shared encoder provides a common latent representation, but the tasks remain acoustically diverse. To reduce negative transfer, we use a Multi-gate Mixture-of-Experts (MMoE) layer \cite{ma2018mmoe} as a task-aware decoder.

The MMoE module contains a pool of eight shared experts. Each expert is a lightweight temporal convolutional block with two sequential 1D convolutional layers of kernel size 3 and length-preserving padding, separated by a ReLU activation. All experts process the shared latent sequence $\mathbf{Z}$ in parallel.

For each task $k$, a dedicated gating network $G_k(\cdot)$ acts as a dynamic router. At time step $t$, the gate takes the latent feature $\mathbf{z}_t$ and outputs a softmax-normalized weight vector $\mathbf{w}_k(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{8}$. The task-specific feature vector is then computed as a weighted combination of expert outputs:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}_{k,t} = \sum_{i=1}^{8} \mathbf{w}_{k,i}(t) \cdot \mathbf{e}_{i,t},
\qquad
\mathbf{w}_k(t) = \operatorname{Softmax}(G_k(\mathbf{z}_t)).
\label{eq:audiodyn_mmoe}
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathbf{e}_{i,t}$ is the output of expert $i$ at time step $t$. Repeating this process frame by frame yields the task-specific sequence $\mathbf{Y}_k = [\mathbf{y}_{k,1}, \dots, \mathbf{y}_{k,T}]^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times 8}$, which is then mapped to logits by a separate linear head for each task.

\subsection{Task-specific post-processing}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_post}

The model outputs frame-wise probabilities, which are converted into discrete musical events through tailored post-processing. For beat and downbeat detection, we adopt the post-processing procedure from \cite{foscarin2024beat}: probabilities are thresholded at 50\%, and peak picking is applied within a 7-frame local neighborhood (radius 3 at 50~fps). For dynamic markings, the dynamic level at each beat is determined by taking the \texttt{argmax} of the class probabilities at that frame. When an external beat grid is available, this sampling step is performed on that grid; otherwise, the model's own beat predictions are used, which gives the fully end-to-end mode considered in this chapter.

Change points are determined in two stages. First, we identify all frames whose change-point probability exceeds 75\%. Second, we snap each candidate to the nearest detected beat. This beat-snapping step is motivated by the annotation style of MazurkaBL, where dynamic markings are beat-aligned rather than freely placed in continuous time \cite{kosta2018mazurkabl}. The step also keeps evaluation consistent with prior work \cite{kosta2017thesis}.

\subsection{Loss function}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_loss}

Training uses a composite multi-task objective,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MTL}} =
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dyn}} +
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CPt}} +
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Beat}} +
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dbt}}.
\label{eq:audiodyn_loss}
\end{equation}

The dynamic head is trained with beat-masked cross-entropy. Let $\mathbf{d}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{C}$ denote the dynamic logits at frame $t$, let $c_t \in \{1,\ldots,C\}$ denote the reference class, and let $m_t^{\mathrm{beat}} \in \{0,1\}$ be a mask that is one only at annotated beat frames. Then
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dyn}}
=
\sum_{t=1}^{T}
m_t^{\mathrm{beat}}\,\mathrm{CE}(\mathbf{d}_t,c_t).
\label{eq:audiodyn_dyn_loss}
\end{equation}
In other words, only beat-aligned frames contribute to the dynamic loss, while inter-beat frames are ignored. This directly reflects the annotation style of MazurkaBL and prevents the dynamic head from learning spurious inter-beat fluctuations between neighbouring beats.

For the three binary tasks, i.e., change points, beats, and downbeats, we adopt the shift-tolerant weighted BCE from Beat This \cite{foscarin2024beat}. We first write the weighted BCE in the familiar form
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{BCE}_{\alpha}(y,\hat{y}) = -\alpha y\log \hat{y} -(1-y)\log(1-\hat{y}),
\label{eq:audiodyn_bce_component}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the positive-class weight. For each binary task $k \in \{\mathrm{CPt},\mathrm{Beat},\mathrm{Dbt}\}$, let $y_t^{(k)} \in \{0,1\}$ and $\hat{y}_t^{(k)} \in (0,1)$ denote the target and predicted probability at frame $t$, and let $\mathcal{P}_{r}(\cdot)$ denote one-dimensional max-pooling over a temporal radius $r$. The shift-tolerant loss is written as
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{k}^{+}
&=
\sum_{t=1}^{T}
y_t^{(k)}\,\mathrm{BCE}_{\alpha_k}\!\left(1,\mathcal{P}_{3}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(k)})_t\right),
\label{eq:audiodyn_shift_bce_pos}\\
\mathcal{L}_{k}^{-}
&=
\sum_{t=1}^{T}
\left(1-\mathcal{P}_{6}(\mathbf{y}^{(k)})_t\right)
\mathrm{BCE}_{\alpha_k}\!\left(0,\mathcal{P}_{3}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(k)})_t\right),
\label{eq:audiodyn_shift_bce_neg}\\
\mathcal{L}_{k}
&=
\mathcal{L}_{k}^{+}+\mathcal{L}_{k}^{-}.
\label{eq:audiodyn_shift_bce}
\end{align}
The positive term applies BCE to the local maximum prediction within a 7-frame neighborhood, so a small temporal shift around the annotation is tolerated. The negative term applies BCE only outside a wider exclusion zone created by $\mathcal{P}_{6}(\mathbf{y}^{(k)})$, so frames close to a positive label are not penalized as hard negatives. This design is useful for beat-like targets because the positive frames are extremely sparse and the annotations are not perfectly precise at frame level. In implementation, the same loss is applied on logits for numerical stability.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:audiodyn_exp}

\subsection{Dataset and protocol}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_data}

We use the MazurkaBL dataset \cite{kosta2018mazurkabl}, a score-aligned corpus of 2{,}098 solo piano recordings covering 44 Chopin Mazurkas. After excluding two mazurkas with irregular dynamics annotations (M06-4 and M63-2), 1{,}999 recordings are used. Although newer datasets such as EME33 \cite{hung2024eme33} exist, MazurkaBL remains the largest publicly available resource for studying notated dynamics against performed loudness from audio. Its provision of score-aligned beat times and verified expressive markings makes it particularly suitable for the task of beat-synchronous score-dynamics transcription.

We use 5-fold cross-validation stratified at the mazurka-opus level. This split is deliberately piece-aware: performances of the same mazurka do not appear in both training and test folds. Given the limited size of the corpus, this provides a data-efficient way to test generalization to unseen pieces within MazurkaBL, while avoiding the overly optimistic estimates that can arise when different performances of the same work leak across splits. For each fold, the model is trained for 120 epochs, and the best checkpoint is selected on the validation set using F1 score. Optimization uses AdamW with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size 10, and fixed random seed 86.

During training, audio is sliced into 60-second segments with 50\% overlap. No overlap is used at evaluation time. Both BSSL and log-Mel features are extracted with the same STFT configuration to ensure identical temporal resolution. The empirically optimal temporal scaling factor is $s=5$. The model predicts six dynamic classes: one \textit{blank} class and the five annotated dynamic levels \textit{pp}, \textit{p}, \textit{mf}, \textit{f}, and \textit{ff}. On an NVIDIA RTX~3090 (24~GiB), a full 5-fold run requires approximately 20 hours, with peak GPU memory usage around 4~GiB.

Beyond this in-domain test, Section~\ref{ssec:audiodyn_cross_dataset} reports a cross-dataset evaluation on the Vienna4x22 dataset \cite{goebl1999vienna4x22}, where models trained on MazurkaBL are applied without fine-tuning.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_baselines}

We compare the proposed model against both single-task baselines and task-specific baselines. These baselines are intentionally heterogeneous: some address only dynamics or change points, while others address only beats and downbeats. Accordingly, the dashes in Table~\ref{tab:audiodyn_main_results} indicate tasks that a baseline does not support, rather than experiments that were omitted.

\paragraph{Single-task multi-scale network.}
We establish single-task baselines by adapting the multi-scale network from \cite{li2023frame}, integrating its public implementation into our data pipeline, and training one independent model per target. Each single-task model keeps the same feature extraction, audio segmentation, encoder depth, optimizer, and post-processing settings as the proposed system, and differs only in that it solves one target at a time instead of learning all four jointly. Each model uses the corresponding task-specific loss from the multi-task objective, with scaling factor $s=5$. The parameter count reported in Table~\ref{tab:audiodyn_main_results} is therefore for one single-task model instance; solving the four tasks independently would require four such models.

\paragraph{Dynamic and change-point baselines.}
For dynamics and change points, we report published results from representative methods in \cite{kosta2017thesis}, namely the Artificial Neural Network (ANN) for dynamics and the Pruned Exact Linear Time (PELT) algorithm for change-point detection. These baselines address only their own target and are not trained jointly with metrical estimation. They are also tuning-intensive and not end-to-end, so a faithful re-implementation would introduce many secondary design choices unrelated to the main contribution of this chapter. We therefore report their published scores directly, as in the ICASSP paper.

\paragraph{Beat and downbeat baselines.}
For beats and downbeats, we include the time-convolutional network with dynamic Bayesian network post-processing \cite{bock2020deconstruct} and the recent state-of-the-art transformer model Beat This \cite{foscarin2024beat}. Both are joint beat/downbeat systems and therefore populate only the beat-related columns in Table~\ref{tab:audiodyn_main_results}. We retrain them from scratch on MazurkaBL using their public implementations and the same 5-fold protocol as ours. To avoid turning these baselines into modified systems, we preserve their original log-Mel front-end and task formulation.

Finally, the rows marked \textit{w/o.\ BSSL} in Table~\ref{tab:audiodyn_main_results} are not additional baselines from the literature. They are feature-control variants of our own architectures in which the 22-band BSSL input is replaced with a 128-bin log-Mel input under otherwise matched settings. This isolates the effect of the front-end from the effect of the model architecture.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_metrics}

All four tasks are evaluated using F1 score. In its standard form,
\begin{align}
P &= \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}},
\label{eq:audiodyn_precision}\\
R &= \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN}},
\label{eq:audiodyn_recall}\\
F_{1} &= \frac{2PR}{P+R}.
\label{eq:audiodyn_f1}
\end{align}
Here, $\mathrm{TP}$, $\mathrm{FP}$, and $\mathrm{FN}$ denote true positives, false positives, and false negatives, respectively.

For beat and downbeat tracking, the evaluation is event-based rather than frame-based. In other words, beats and downbeats are treated as binary time-stamped events, not as continuous labeled regions. We therefore count matches by one-to-one event alignment: a predicted beat or downbeat is a true positive only if it can be matched to an unmatched reference event within a $\pm 70$~ms window; otherwise it is a false positive, and any unmatched reference event becomes a false negative. We keep this $\pm 70$~ms tolerance because it is the standard F1 setting used by recent beat-tracking work and by \texttt{mir\_eval}, which makes the present results directly comparable with the literature \cite{raffel2014mireval,bock2020deconstruct,foscarin2024beat}. It should therefore be read as an evaluation convention rather than a musically fundamental constant.

Dynamics and change points follow the evaluation protocol of \cite{kosta2017thesis}. For dynamics, the frame-wise output of the dynamic head is sampled at each ground-truth beat location and then discretized into score labels. This turns the problem into beat-wise multi-class classification on the same metrical grid as the annotations. Let $\mathcal{C}=\{\textit{pp},\textit{p},\textit{mf},\textit{f},\textit{ff}\}$ denote the five musically meaningful classes, excluding the \textit{blank} class. We compute class-wise F1 as
\begin{equation}
F_{1,c}
=
\frac{2\,\mathrm{TP}_{c}}{2\,\mathrm{TP}_{c}+\mathrm{FP}_{c}+\mathrm{FN}_{c}},
\qquad c \in \mathcal{C},
\label{eq:audiodyn_classwise_f1}
\end{equation}
and report the macro-average
\begin{equation}
F_{1}^{\mathrm{Dyn}}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}|}
\sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}} F_{1,c}.
\label{eq:audiodyn_macro_f1}
\end{equation}
The \textit{blank} class is excluded because it only covers the region before the first annotated marking and is not itself a score-dynamics symbol.

For change points, the predicted times are first snapped to the nearest ground-truth beat before evaluation. The task then reduces to binary classification on beat indices: a predicted change point is counted as correct only if its snapped beat index exactly matches a reference change-point index. Since both dynamics and change points are evaluated on the same beat grid as the annotations, no additional timing tolerance is required for these two tasks.

The multi-task formulation is consistently better than the corresponding single-task counterpart under the same BSSL front-end. Relative to the single-task BSSL model, the proposed multi-task model improves dynamic F1 by 3.8 points, change-point F1 by 5.1 points, beat F1 by 0.1 points, and downbeat F1 by 10.2 points. These gains indicate that joint learning is useful rather than harmful in this setting, provided that the shared representation is combined with task-aware decoding.

The feature comparison also reveals an important trade-off. BSSL is better for dynamics, change points, and beats, whereas log-Mel remains slightly better for downbeats. The practical advantage of BSSL lies in its compactness: the input dimensionality drops from 128 mel bins to 22 Bark bands, reducing the model size from 14.7~M parameters to 0.5~M in the multi-task setting. This drastic reduction enables much longer input sequences and makes long-context training feasible on modest hardware.

This result should not be read as a dimensionality effect alone. In the terms of Chapter~\ref{ch:background}, BSSL preserves the Bark-wise distribution of specific loudness after auditory weighting and masking, whereas a scalar loudness curve would discard that structure and a conventional log-Mel spectrogram does not encode it explicitly. The gains in dynamic and change-point prediction are therefore consistent with the thesis-level claim that score-dynamics recovery benefits from a representation closer to perceived loudness than to raw spectral magnitude.


\subsection{Ablation study}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_ablation}

To validate the design choices of the proposed model, we conduct an extended ablation study against the full BSSL configuration. The full model uses the MMoE decoder, temporal scaling with $s=5$, data augmentation, and 60-second audio segments. Table~\ref{tab:audiodyn_ablation} evaluates the architecture across three dimensions: the removal of key model components, the variation of the temporal scaling factor $s$, and the adjustment of the input audio segment length. This organisation is deliberate because it directly reflects our core modeling hypothesis. The component ablations show that all major parts of the model contribute to the final performance, but not in the same way. The most diagnostic result is the removal of temporal scaling. Setting $s=1$ reduces the average F1 from 55.0 to 46.5, with particularly large drops in change-point F1 and downbeat F1. This indicates that the performance gain does not merely come from using a deeper convolutional encoder, but from exposing the downstream tasks to genuinely different temporal resolutions. Removing MMoE also degrades all four tasks, supporting the view that the four heads have different preference on the same shared latent. The overlap-based data augmentation gives a further improvement, which is expected for this dataset because change points and downbeats are sparse events. Overlapping audio segments increase the chance that such events appear in varied local contexts during training.

The temporal-scaling results give a detailed view of the multi-scale encoder. The reference architecture of \cite{li2023frame} uses a scaling factor of 3, but this value is not optimal for our tasks combination. With $s=3$, beat F1 and change-point F1 remain strong, but the average score is lower than the full model. Increasing the factor to $s=5$ gives the best overall balance. A slightly larger value, $s=7$, remains competitive and gives the second-best average score, but further increasing the factor to $s=9$ causes a clear degradation, especially for downbeats. This pattern suggests that the model needs a sufficiently coarse branch to capture long-range dynamic context, but excessive temporal compression begins to remove information that cannot be fully recovered for event localization. The best value is therefore not simply the largest receptive field, but the best compromise between contextual abstraction and temporal precision.

The segment-length results further support this interpretation. The 30-second setting is included because it is a common choice in beat/downbeat tracking systems, including Beat This \cite{foscarin2024beat}. It performs reasonably well for beat and downbeat tracking, but is clearly weaker for dynamics and change points. Moving from 30 seconds to 60 seconds improves dynamic F1 from 49.1 to 54.4 and change-point F1 from 19.2 to 26.1, confirming that dynamics-related targets benefit from musical context beyond the short excerpts typically used for metrical tracking. However, the trend is not monotonic. The 90-second setting achieves the best dynamic and change-point F1 scores, but its downbeat F1 drops sharply, reducing the overall average. The 120-second setting is worse than 60 seconds on all four tasks. These results show that longer audio context is helpful for dynamics, but only up to the point where the joint model can still maintain stable event localization. We therefore use 60-second segments as the default setting, not because it maximizes every individual task, but because it gives the best multi-task trade-off across dynamics, change points, beats, and downbeats.

\subsection{Discussion: why score dynamics is not a relabelled MIDI velocity problem}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_discussion}

This chapter makes a different thesis-level point from Chapters~\ref{ch:velcomp} and \ref{ch:scoreinf}. The target is not note-level control, and the output is not intended to drive a synthesizer directly. Instead, the objective is a score-facing summary of expressive intensity on a musically meaningful coordinate. Score dynamics is therefore not obtained here by simply discretizing an underlying velocity curve. It is learned as a symbolic description of dynamic regimes together with the metrical structure needed to place that description on a score.

This distinction explains several design choices. The model uses longer context because phrase-level dynamic intent extends beyond local note attacks. The post-processing snaps change points to beats because the available annotations are editorial and metrical rather than freely continuous. The evaluation is beat-synchronous because a musically meaningful score label should be judged on the score grid, not only on raw frame precision.

The chapter also complements Thread~A rather than replacing it. Note-level velocity is the more precise target when the goal is editing or rendering. Beat-synchronous score dynamics is the more interpretable target when the goal is symbolic annotation, educational feedback, or performance analysis. The thesis requires both views in order to connect machine-readable control with human-readable musical description.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{ssec:audiodyn_limits}

Several limitations should be kept in mind. First, the dynamic vocabulary is restricted to the five static score levels present in MazurkaBL plus a \textit{blank} class. The model does not explicitly represent longer-span symbols such as \textit{crescendo} and \textit{diminuendo}; instead, it captures local transitions through change points. Second, the beat-aligned post-processing reflects the annotation style of MazurkaBL. This is musically sensible for this dataset, but it may not transfer directly to corpora with different editorial conventions.

Third, the experiments are limited to solo piano recordings, and the repertoire is concentrated on Chopin mazurkas. The chapter therefore demonstrates that audio-to-score dynamics transcription is feasible in a controlled but musically meaningful setting, rather than claiming immediate generalization to other instruments or styles. Finally, the feature comparison suggests that no single front-end is uniformly optimal across all tasks: BSSL is the better global choice for the present chapter, but downbeat tracking still benefits from log-Mel input.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:audiodyn_summary}

This chapter established a compact multi-task, multi-scale framework for recovering beat-synchronous score dynamics directly from piano audio. Using Bark-scale specific loudness as input and MMoE as task-aware decoding, the model jointly predicts dynamic levels, change points, beats, and downbeats while remaining highly parameter-efficient. On MazurkaBL, the framework achieves strong benchmark results and shows that long-context, perceptually grounded audio modeling is a practical route from performance audio to score-level dynamic annotations. Within the broader thesis, this chapter provides the Thread~B counterpart to the velocity-focused methods of the preceding chapters, addresses Requirement~R4 most directly through its beat-synchronous output design, and completes the audio-only scenario of expressive label recovery.
