A structurally perfect sequence of notes is only the foundation of a musical performance. When musicians receive a composed score, they do not merely execute a rigid sequence of instructions. They interpret the piece and bring it to life. A central dimension of this interpretation is musical dynamics. This involves the deliberate shaping of loudness over individual notes, phrases, and larger formal units. Dynamics conveys emotional nuance. It balances voices inside a texture and shapes the direction of a phrase. It is often the clearest signal that separates a convincing human performance from a flat mechanical rendering.

In the digital domain, musical performance is commonly represented in two complementary symbolic forms. The first is MIDI, where note events are paired with note-level control parameters such as velocity. MIDI velocity records the action intensity at note onset. It is precise, machine-readable, and directly editable. The second is the music score, where dynamics is represented by human-readable markings such as \textit{pp}, \textit{p}, \textit{mf}, \textit{f}, \textit{crescendo}, and \textit{diminuendo}. These markings are less precise as physical measurements, but they communicate musical intent on a musically meaningful metrical grid. Modern music workflows need both representations. MIDI supports production, rendering, and downstream learning. Scores remain the standard interface for analysis, education, and communication between musicians.

Automatic Music Transcription (AMT) aims to bridge acoustic performance and symbolic music representations. Driven by deep learning and large-scale datasets \cite{hawthorne2019maestro}, recent AMT systems can transcribe note events with high accuracy. They can increasingly recover note-level velocity as well \cite{hawthorne2018onsets,kong2021hpt,toyama2023hft}. Yet this progress remains asymmetric \cite{amt2019ov}. The field is highly proficient at recovering what notes are played and when they occur. It is much less mature at recovering how loud those notes are played in terms of the dynamics shaping.

This thesis takes the stance that dynamics should be treated as a first-class target in expressive AMT. A structurally correct transcription without dynamics is still limited for many real applications. It often defaults to flat note intensity. It does not support expressive rendering. It is also insufficient for performance feedback, expressive analysis, or the curation of performance-ready symbolic corpora. At the same time, this thesis does not attempt full expressive performance modelling. It focuses on dynamics because dynamics is the most practically editable and perceptually salient expressive dimension, and because it can be recovered while preserving the original note content and note timing.


\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Transcribing correct pitches and timing is only the foundational step of AMT. To recover the complete musical intent, dynamics must be treated as a first-class transcription target. This thesis is driven by a critical need: recovering musically interpretable dynamics from existing corpora to produce expressive, editable symbolic representations. This goal addresses three practical challenges:

\textbf{MIDI production and expressive rendering.} 
MIDI is the dominant representation in digital music production. It supports efficient editing and flexible rendering. Yet, a vast amount of software-generated MIDI is created via step input or quantization, leaving velocity at a lifeless, default constant. Manual velocity editing is time-consuming and delicate. Creators often want to preserve their composed notes and timing; they simply need to inject human-like expression. This motivates symbolic-only velocity completion as a separable, essential layer for realistic music rendering.

\textbf{Music education and performance feedback.} 
Dynamics is a fundamental pedagogical dimension. Teachers rely on it to shape phrases, highlight accents, and balance voices. In music education, a basic score is often available alongside a student's audio performance. However, existing audio-only AMT systems struggle to leverage this prior score information. Developing lightweight, score-informed correction mechanisms is crucial. Reliable dynamics estimates finally enable automatic feedback systems to evaluate performances far beyond mere pitch and timing \cite{kim2022piano}.

\textbf{Expressive data curation and semantic transcription.} 
Many large-scale music archives provide aligned audio and symbolic content. However, expressive labels are consistently missing. Velocities are flattened; score dynamic markings are absent or unreliable. This severely limits the value of these corpora for performance analysis and machine learning. Furthermore, a deep semantic gap exists between physical machine measurements (MIDI velocity) and subjective human cognition (score dynamics). Bridging this gap—and extending it beyond the piano—is imperative. Recovering these missing dimensions upgrades static archives into richly expressive, performance-ready datasets.

Driven by these challenges, this research develops a comprehensive label-recovery framework. By advancing from note-level velocity estimation to beat-level score dynamics transcription, this thesis elevates AMT from a mere structural transcription tool into a complete pipeline for expressive musical curation.

\section{Research Gap and Thesis Stance}
\label{sec:intro:gap_stance}

In many realistic corpora, some symbolic information is already available, but the expressive layer is incomplete. A MIDI file may contain correct note pitches and timings while leaving all velocities at a default constant. An aligned score may exist for a recording, but its dynamics may be sparse or unavailable. A performance recording may exist without either reliable velocity labels or score-level dynamic annotations. These are not unusual corner cases. They are common in digital production, music pedagogy, online music archives, and dataset construction.

The difficulty of this family of problems can be summarized in four gaps.

\paragraph{Representation mismatch.}
Audio loudness, MIDI velocity, and score dynamics are related, but they are not identical. Loudness is a perceptual phenomenon. MIDI velocity is a note-level control signal. Score dynamics is a symbolic and context-dependent notation of musical intention. A method that moves from one layer to another must therefore solve more than a simple label conversion.

\paragraph{Annotation ambiguity.}
Score dynamics is sparse, relative, and edition-dependent. MIDI velocity is precise within one instrument or capture chain, but it is not universally comparable across instruments and renderers. Even when annotations exist, they do not all have the same semantic certainty. This makes expressive AMT harder than note transcription.

\paragraph{Supervision fragmentation.}
Different datasets support different claims. Some provide audio and reliable note-level velocity. Some provide audio and aligned score. Some provide beat-level score annotations without note-level performance control. A useful thesis on expressive dynamics should therefore not assume one ideal dataset regime. It should address several realistic supervision settings explicitly.

\paragraph{Cross-dataset and cross-instrument fragility.}
Velocity estimation is substantially more fragile than note transcription under changes in instrument, room acoustics, recording chain, and alignment quality. Beyond piano, the problem becomes even harder because reliable note-level velocity labels are scarce. In that case the bottleneck is not only domain shift. It is missing supervision.

This thesis studies that family of problems through a unified \emph{label-recovery} perspective. The objective is not to compose new music. It is not to generate a complete expressive performance from scratch. It is not to rewrite the note sequence. It is not to perform style transfer. Instead, the objective is to recover dynamics labels that are missing, flattened, noisy, weakly represented, or available only through proxy supervision. The recovered labels should remain editable. They should remain musically interpretable. Most importantly, they should not require changing the note content or note timing that is already present in the data.

This stance is especially important for a paper-based dissertation. The thesis does not claim one mature end-to-end pipeline. The chapters are stand-alone studies with different assumptions, datasets, and outputs. Their unity is conceptual rather than architectural. Each chapter asks how a musically interpretable dynamics label can be recovered under a particular supervision bottleneck. Chapters~3--5 form the core scenario arc of the thesis. Chapter~6 is a deliberate exploratory extension that probes the beyond-piano frontier when direct velocity labels are unavailable.

\section{Dynamics as a Target in Expressive AMT}
\label{sec:intro:why_dynamics}

Dynamics is chosen as the first expressive target for three reasons.

\paragraph{Perceptual salience.}
Dynamics is one of the most immediately audible dimensions of expressive performance. Listeners often perceive phrase direction, accent, and balance through intensity change before they perceive subtler aspects such as pedaling or micro-timing. A transcription that omits dynamics therefore omits a central part of musical meaning.

\paragraph{Symbolic editability.}
Dynamics is also one of the easiest expressive dimensions to edit in symbolic workflows. MIDI velocity is a standard control parameter in digital audio workstations. Score dynamics is a standard notational device in composition, pedagogy, and analysis. This makes dynamics an unusually strong bridge between MIR outputs and real musical practice.

\paragraph{Recoverability across realistic data conditions.}
Some expressive targets require fine control of instrument-specific mechanisms that are poorly labeled in current corpora. Dynamics is difficult, but it remains recoverable in several useful settings: symbolic-only completion, score-informed refinement, score-facing transcription from audio, and weakly supervised exploratory transfer beyond piano. This makes it an especially strong first target for a thesis that aims to move expressive AMT beyond note content alone.

This focus does not imply that articulation, pedaling, timbre, or micro-timing are unimportant. They matter deeply. The present claim is narrower. Dynamics is the first expressive target because it is simultaneously audible, editable, and recoverable across multiple realistic data conditions.

\section{Two Complementary Forms of Dynamics}
\label{sec:intro:two_forms}

The thesis studies two complementary symbolic forms of musical dynamics. They are closely related, but they operate at different musical interfaces.

\paragraph{MIDI velocity.}
MIDI velocity is a note-level control parameter. It is precise, compact, and useful in production workflows. It is the right target when the goal is expressive rendering, note-level editing, or post-processing of existing MIDI. For these reasons, it is the direct target of Chapters~3, 4, and the exploratory Chapter~6.

\paragraph{Score dynamics.}
Score dynamics is a human-readable symbolic layer. It represents dynamic intention on a musically structured grid through markings such as \textit{p}, \textit{mf}, \textit{f}, \textit{crescendo}, and \textit{diminuendo}. It is the right target when the goal is score augmentation, pedagogical feedback, or musically interpretable score-facing transcription. For these reasons, it is the direct target of Chapter~5.

The unity of the thesis does not come from forcing all expressive information into one output format. It comes from treating both note-level velocity and score-level dynamics as legitimate targets inside one broader family of label-recovery problems.

\section{Unified Thesis Question and Three Core Scenarios}
\label{sec:intro:question}

The thesis is driven by the following question:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{How can musically interpretable dynamics labels be recovered from incomplete, weakly expressive, or weakly supervised music data without rewriting note content or note timing?}
\end{quote}

That question is studied under three core scenarios.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{The three core supervision scenarios studied in this thesis.}
\label{tab:intro_scenarios}
\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l X X X X}
\toprule
\textbf{Scenario} & \textbf{Available data} & \textbf{Missing label} & \textbf{Target output} & \textbf{Main chapter} \\
\midrule
S1 & symbolic MIDI note content and timing & note-level expressive intensity & MIDI velocity completion & Chapter~3 \\
S2 & performance audio + aligned score + preliminary AMT output & reliable note-level expressive intensity & score-informed MIDI velocity refinement & Chapter~4 \\
S3 & performance audio only & human-readable score-facing dynamics and metrical coordinates & beat-synchronous dynamic levels, change points, beats, downbeats & Chapter~5 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Scenario S1: symbolic-only MIDI velocity completion.} The note content and timing are already available in MIDI form, but the velocity is missing or flattened. The goal is to complete note-level velocity from symbolic structure alone.

    \item \textbf{Scenario S2: audio-plus-score velocity refinement.} A performance recording is available together with an aligned score. The score provides stable note content and timing, while the AMT-estimated velocity remains noisy. The goal is to refine note-level MIDI velocity by using the score as a structural prior.

    \item \textbf{Scenario S3: audio-only score-dynamics recovery.} Only the performance audio is available, and the goal is not raw note-level velocity but beat-synchronous score dynamics. The system must therefore recover dynamic levels together with the metrical structure needed to place them on a symbolic score.
\end{enumerate}

These three scenarios are intentionally ordered from the most controlled setting to the most open one. Scenario S1 isolates dynamics recovery from other uncertainties by assuming that symbolic notes are already available. Scenario S2 adds real audio and score context, but keeps reliable structural support. Scenario S3 removes that support and moves to a more human-readable but more ambiguous target.

The thesis also contains a fourth study in Chapter~6. That chapter is \emph{not} presented as a fourth core scenario in the same sense. Instead, it is an exploratory extension of the note-level velocity thread. It asks whether note-level velocity learning can move beyond piano when true labels are scarce and proxy supervision through differentiable synthesis becomes necessary.

\section{Aim, Scope, and Assumptions}
\label{sec:intro:scope}

The aim of this thesis is to develop stand-alone but conceptually linked methods for recovering dynamics-related labels from real music data. The recovered labels include note-level MIDI velocity, beat-synchronous dynamic levels, change points, beats, and downbeats. The intended outcome is not only stronger AMT-style prediction, but also more expressive and more editable symbolic music for downstream use.

The main scope and assumptions are as follows.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Primary empirical focus.} The main experimental focus is piano. This is because reliable note-level velocity ground truth remains concentrated on instrumented pianos particularly by the Yamaha Disklavier systems \cite{hawthorne2019maestro}.

    \item \textbf{Preservation of note content and timing.} The thesis does not aim to rewrite the note sequence. Dynamics is treated as a refinement or annotation layer on top of fixed note events.

    \item \textbf{Two complementary outputs.} The thesis studies both note-level MIDI velocity and beat-synchronous score dynamics. It does not force all expressive information into a single representation.

    \item \textbf{Out-of-scope factors.} The thesis does not attempt full modelling of articulation, pedal, timbre, or micro-timing, except when these factors provide indirect context for dynamics recovery.

    \item \textbf{Beyond piano.} Extending velocity estimation beyond piano is recognized as an important direction because piano-centric datasets create a real bias in expressive AMT. However, large-scale non-piano score-dynamics transcription with human labels remains outside the present thesis scope.
\end{itemize}

\section{Main Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:contrib}

The main contributions of this thesis are as follows.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A thesis-level formulation of dynamics-oriented label recovery.} The dissertation argues that dynamics should be treated as a first-class target in expressive AMT and organizes the problem around heterogeneous supervision regimes rather than one ideal dataset.

    \item \textbf{A symbolic-only method for note-level velocity completion.} Chapter~3 reformulates MIDI velocity completion as an image-colorization problem on sparse piano-roll representations. It introduces a U-Net with window attention and a sparsity-aware loss, and shows gains in both objective metrics and listening tests \cite{he2025filling}.

    \item \textbf{A modular score-informed correction strategy for AMT velocity estimation.} Chapter~4 introduces a lightweight Transformer correction module that refines AMT-estimated velocity using an aligned score. The design goal is portability across existing AMT backbones rather than a fully bespoke score-informed system.

    \item \textbf{An audio-only framework for beat-synchronous score-dynamics recovery.} Chapter~5 proposes a compact multi-task multi-scale network that jointly estimates dynamic levels, change points, beats, and downbeats. It combines a perceptually motivated Bark-scale specific loudness front-end with task-aware decoding through MMoE \cite{he2026dynest,ma2018mmoe}.

    \item \textbf{An exploratory beyond-piano study based on proxy supervision.} Chapter~6 investigates cross-instrument MIDI velocity estimation when true labels are scarce. It uses differentiable MIDI-to-audio synthesis as part of the supervision signal. The contribution is honest and exploratory: it tests a route beyond piano without claiming a finished universal pipeline.
\end{itemize}

\section{Paper-Based Structure and Publications Map}
\label{sec:intro:pubs}
This thesis contains three core paper-based method chapters. Chapter~\ref{ch:velcomp} is based on the CMMR 2025 paper on symbolic MIDI velocity completion. Chapter~\ref{ch:scoreinf} is based on the SMC 2026 paper on score-informed AMT velocity refinement. Chapter~\ref{ch:audiodyn} is based on the ICASSP 2026 paper on joint estimation of piano dynamics and metrical structure. In each case, the thesis chapter preserves the technical core of the paper while expanding the motivation, task-specific related work, and thesis-facing discussion.

Chapter~6 is different in status. It is a thesis-oriented exploratory chapter rather than a claim of a mature end-to-end system. Its role is to confront the strongest limitation exposed by the earlier chapters: the concentration of reliable note-level velocity labels on piano.

\label{sec:intro:organization}

The remainder of the thesis is organized as follows.

\textbf{Chapter~2} establishes the shared conceptual system of the dissertation. It defines what dynamics means across audio, MIDI, and score. It reviews the two main literature threads of the thesis. It also summarizes datasets, evaluation strategies, and the thesis requirements that motivate the later chapters.

\textbf{Chapter~3} addresses Scenario~S1. It studies symbolic-only MIDI velocity completion. It shows that note-level dynamics can be recovered as a controllable post-processing layer while keeping the original note content and timing fixed.

\textbf{Chapter~4} addresses Scenario~S2. It studies score-informed velocity refinement from performance audio. It shows that an aligned score can act as a portable structural prior for AMT-estimated velocity and can improve robustness under dataset shift.

\textbf{Chapter~5} addresses Scenario~S3. It studies audio-only recovery of beat-synchronous score dynamics. It shows that score-facing dynamic labels can be estimated jointly with the metrical structure needed to place them on a symbolic score.

\textbf{Chapter~6} extends the note-level velocity thread beyond piano. It studies cross-instrument MIDI velocity estimation with proxy supervision. The chapter is framed explicitly as exploratory because the supervision problem changes once direct velocity labels disappear.

\textbf{Chapter~7} revisits the unified thesis question, synthesizes the findings across the four studies, states the limitations clearly, and outlines future directions for expressive AMT.
